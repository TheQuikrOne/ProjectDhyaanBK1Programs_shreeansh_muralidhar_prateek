## 1. Analysis Pipeline

Raw segmented EEG trials were bandpass filtered (4th-order Butterworth, zero-phase), downsampled, and concatenated across good trials to form continuous electrode-time trajectories. Trials were concatenated rather than averaged because spontaneous oscillations are not phase-locked to trial onset and would cancel under averaging. Bad trials were rejected using a three-layer pipeline inherited from the Biswas et al. preprocessing code: (1) eye position excursions, (2) RMS amplitude thresholding (1.5-35 uV), and (3) PSD outlier detection (mean + 6 SD). Minimum thresholds: 30 good trials and 10 good electrodes per subject-protocol.

The analysis framework had three tiers:

**Tier 1 — Signal Subspace Extraction:** PCA was applied to each subject's electrode-time matrix. Horn's Parallel Analysis (100 permutations, 95th percentile threshold) determined the individual embedding dimension k. The null distribution is generated by **Phase Randomization** of each electrode's FFT (with conjugate symmetry preserved so the IFFT is real), producing colored-noise surrogates that match the bandpassed power spectrum of the data. This replaces the earlier time-shuffling (`randperm`) approach, which destroyed autocorrelation and produced white-noise surrogates that artificially lowered the threshold and inflated k. A global standardized dimension K_std was set as the median k of Controls at EO1 baseline. All subsequent linear metrics were computed in this common K_std-dimensional space to enable fair cross-subject comparison of volume.

**Tier 2 — Dynamical Metrics:** Four markers were computed in the K_std-dimensional PCA space:
- **Trajectory Velocity:** Mean Euclidean step size between consecutive *within-trial* time points (proxy for state volatility). Step sizes spanning the boundary between concatenated trials are masked out before averaging — these would otherwise inject artificial "teleportation" spikes from the discontinuity between the last sample of trial N and the first sample of trial N+1.
- **State Space Volume:** Log hyper-ellipsoid volume from eigenvalues of the projected covariance (quantifies dynamic range). Eigenvalues are clamped at a floor of 1e-6 (rather than MATLAB's `eps` ≈ 2.2×10⁻¹⁶), so subjects with locally low-rank covariance are penalized gracefully rather than catastrophically dragged into a -36 hole by `log(eps)`.
- **Excursion Radius:** Mean distance of trajectory points from the geometric centroid (measures spatial spread).
- **Participation Ratio (PR):** PR = (Σλ)² / Σλ², computed from the full PCA eigenvalue spectrum. PR is an amplitude-independent measure of effective linear dimensionality: PR = 1 when all variance is in one component, PR = N when variance is distributed uniformly across N components. Unlike volume/velocity/radius, PR is a pure ratio and is not inflated by total signal power.

**Tier 3 — Intrinsic Complexity:** The Two-Nearest Neighbors (TNN) estimator (Facco et al., 2017) was applied to the full-dimensional data (not k-truncated) to estimate nonlinear intrinsic dimension d. If full dimensionality caused memory constraints, PCA retaining 99% of variance was applied first. Data was subsampled to 10,000 points via uniform random sampling when trajectory length exceeded this threshold.

**Statistics:** All between-group comparisons used paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests (matched meditator-control pairs). Within-group EO1 vs M1 comparisons also used paired Wilcoxon signed-rank. To disentangle structural expansion from amplitude inflation in the volume metric, an **ANCOVA** (multiple linear regression) was additionally fit per protocol: `LogVolume ~ Group + TotalVariance`, with Group as a categorical predictor (Med = 1, Con = 0) and TotalVariance (sum of all PCA eigenvalues, a proxy for total signal power) as a continuous covariate. The p-value on the Group coefficient tests whether the volume difference between meditators and controls survives controlling for power. Significance thresholds: * p<0.05, ** p<0.01, *** p<0.005.

**Important note on metric interpretation:** Velocity, volume, and radius are amplitude-dependent — they scale with signal power. A signal that is 2x louder will produce approximately 2x velocity and 2x radius regardless of dynamical complexity. The embedding dimension k, Participation Ratio (PR), and TNN intrinsic dimension d are amplitude-independent — they measure the number of independent degrees of freedom irrespective of signal magnitude. This distinction is critical for interpreting group differences: k, PR, d, and the ANCOVA-adjusted volume all test the dimensionality / structural hypothesis directly, while raw velocity/volume/radius confound dynamical structure with power.

### 3.4 Gamma Summary

The amplitude-dependent metrics (velocity, volume, radius) show robust group differences at M1 (all p ≤ 0.013), with meditators exhibiting larger, faster, and more expansive trajectories. These metrics are confounded with gamma power, and Biswas et al. already established that meditators have higher gamma power. However, the ANCOVA result demonstrates that **the LogVolume group difference is not fully reducible to power** — Group remains a significant predictor (p = 0.0011 at M1) even after adjusting for TotalVariance. Consistent with this, the amplitude-independent **Participation Ratio shows a Med > Con trend at M1** (p = 0.065), suggesting meditators distribute variance across more effective dimensions during meditation. The nonlinear intrinsic dimension (TNN d) shows no group difference (p > 0.7), indicating the *manifold topology* is unchanged even as the *linear effective dimensionality* trends apart.

Within-group, the meditation state primarily increases state space volume in both groups (Med p = 0.006, Con p = 0.032). Other metrics show only modest within-group changes after correcting the velocity calculation.

The "Shape of Stillness" hypothesis predicted that meditation would induce a collapse of neural degrees of freedom: lower trajectory velocity, smaller state space volume, reduced excursion radius, and lower intrinsic dimensionality in meditators compared to controls, particularly during M1.

## 7. Interpretation

### 7.1 Gamma band: Amplitude *and* structural expansion, not dimensional collapse

The gamma results are consistent with the established finding that experienced meditators exhibit elevated broadband gamma power (Biswas et al., 2024). The state space analysis adds that this power difference manifests as faster, more expansive neural trajectories. Crucially, the **ANCOVA result rules out the interpretation that the volume expansion is purely a power artifact**: even after regressing out TotalVariance, Group remains a highly significant predictor of LogVolume (p = 0.0011 at M1, p = 0.026 at EO1), and the Group effect more than doubles from EO1 to M1. The Participation Ratio, which is mathematically immune to amplitude scaling, shows the same pattern in trend form (Med > Con at M1, p = 0.065). Together, these indicate that meditators distribute gamma variance across slightly more effective dimensions during meditation — a *structural* expansion on top of the well-established power expansion. The nonlinear manifold topology (TNN d ~ 18 in both groups) remains unchanged, so this is a redistribution of variance within the existing manifold rather than a recruitment of new nonlinear degrees of freedom.

### 7.2 Alpha band: Nonlinear complexity increase, not collapse — and purely nonlinear

The alpha results challenge the hypothesis most directly. The one amplitude-independent metric that does separate the groups — TNN intrinsic dimension at M1 — goes in the opposite direction: meditators show higher nonlinear complexity (d = 12.18) than controls (d = 10.86) during meditation. Furthermore, within meditators, TNN d increases from 10.78 at baseline to 12.18 during meditation (p < 0.0001), while controls show a smaller increase (10.25 to 10.86, p = 0.014).

The upgraded pipeline sharpens this picture. The new alpha **ANCOVA shows no power-adjusted group difference** in volume (Group p = 0.74 at M1, with the coefficient even slightly negative — Med below Con). The new alpha **Participation Ratio is also at chance** in both protocols (p > 0.4) and changes negligibly within-group between EO1 and M1 (p > 0.3). So the alpha group difference is *purely a nonlinear phenomenon* — it is invisible to every linear measure (raw means, ANCOVA-adjusted volume, PR) and appears only in the nonlinear intrinsic-dimension estimator.

This suggests that meditation does not simplify the alpha manifold but instead enriches it in a way that cannot be captured by linear PCA-based measures. Experienced practitioners may recruit more independent alpha generators or engage in more complex patterns of alpha synchronization/desynchronization during focused attention — patterns that fold the trajectory through additional nonlinear directions without changing how variance is distributed across linear PCA components.

### 7.3 Band-specific dissociation

The most notable finding is the dissociation between bands:

- **Gamma:** Group differences are largely amplitude-driven (power), but a *residual linear-structural component* remains after power adjustment (ANCOVA Group p = 0.0011 at M1) and is mirrored by a PR trend (p = 0.065). Nonlinear topology (TNN d) is unchanged. The meditation state preferentially amplifies gamma in meditators, *and* slightly enriches its linear effective dimensionality.
- **Alpha:** No linear group differences exist on any measure — raw amplitude metrics, power-adjusted volume (ANCOVA), and PR are all at chance. The sole group difference is *nonlinear* (TNN d), with meditators showing increased intrinsic dimension. The meditation state activates alpha dynamics universally on linear measures, with controls showing equal or stronger amplitude changes.

This is a crisp band-by-mechanism dissociation. Gamma differences are linear-structural (variance gets redistributed across PCA components, plus more total power). Alpha differences are nonlinear-structural (the manifold develops more independent directions in a way only TNN can detect, with no linear PCA fingerprint at all). Neither band shows the predicted dimensional collapse.

### 7.4 Alternative framing

Rather than a "collapse of degrees of freedom," long-term meditation practice may produce an "expansion of controlled complexity" — the ability to sustain more independent neural modes simultaneously during focused attention, particularly in the alpha band (TNN d), while also generating stronger gamma-band activity that itself is modestly more dimensionally distributed (gamma PR trend, ANCOVA Group effect on volume). The meditator's brain during meditation is not simpler than a control's; it is more richly structured both in the linear sense (more equally distributed PCA variance in gamma) and in the nonlinear sense (higher TNN intrinsic dimension in alpha).

During meditation, the gamma Velocity and Radius results are highly consistent at the pair level: 22 out of 29 matched pairs (75.9%) show the meditator having higher values than their matched control, and the binomial test against 50/50 yields p = 0.008 for both. Log Volume shows the same direction (19/29, 65.5%) but does not cross the per-pair binomial threshold despite being significant at the group level — indicating the volume effect is more about *magnitude per pair* than *consistency across pairs*. The PR trend from §3.1 (group p = 0.065) is mirrored at the pair level (18/29 = 62.1%, binomial p = 0.265), suggesting the structural-expansion signal is real but smaller than the amplitude effects. The k and TNN d counts are at chance, confirming no directional consistency on dimensionality metrics in gamma.

### 8.5 Summary

## 9. Limitations

- Parallel analysis used 100 permutations (reduced from 1000 for computational feasibility); k estimates may have slightly higher variance. Surrogates are now generated by Phase Randomization (preserves the bandpassed power spectrum), which is more conservative than the prior time-shuffle approach and reduced K_std in gamma from 6 to 5.
- TNN subsampling to 10,000 points via uniform random sampling introduces stochasticity; results may vary slightly across runs.
- Velocity, volume, and radius are confounded with signal amplitude and cannot distinguish power differences from dynamical structure differences. The ANCOVA on volume (§3.3) and the Participation Ratio partially address this; k and TNN d provide fully amplitude-independent tests of the dimensionality hypothesis.
- Two subject-protocol combinations were excluded in both analyses (019CKa M1: insufficient good trials; 053DR EO1: missing data).
- The alpha TNN d group difference (p = 0.037) would not survive Bonferroni correction across the between-group tests per band. It should be interpreted as exploratory.
- The gamma PR trend at M1 (p = 0.065) likewise does not cross α = 0.05 and should be treated as suggestive.
- The analysis does not address broadband or cross-frequency dynamics, which may be relevant to the hypothesis.
